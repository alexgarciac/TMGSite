Good afternoon. My name is Ken Brooks.  I’m President of Publishing Dimensions, and formerly VP of Digital Content with Barnes & Noble. Publishing Dimensions’ mission is to connect publishers to digital revenue by providing one stop shopping of conversion of paper and electronic books into all formats of eBooks and POD and placement of those titles in channels of distribution
Subject: I’m here today to discuss Print-on-Demand – an area of publishing where I believe there is significant short-term revenue, but still a good amount of confusion and open issues. Rather than focus on the world of central POD – or “big iron” POD – I’m going to discuss distributed POD – call it “in-store” or “small iron” POD.

Transition:  Before getting into all of that, let's look at some of the open issues with distributed POD.
Now I’m not going to read all of these, but I think you get the point.  Distributed POD is a very interesting model that’s showing a great deal of promise in the few locations where it is installed.  But there are still questions to be resolved. What is the consumer experience?  How good is the quality of reproduction? How necessary is standardization? 
Economics of one-off production:  is it even feasible?
Books are low volume merchandise at store level.  How big does the digital library need to be to achieve sufficient volume? Doesn’t central POD provide better economics at *any* scale?  Why not use central POD for everything?  Should this be internally developed or outsourced? Traffic patterns in store are highly variable over the course of a day and a year.  What happens to waiting time for a customer's book?
Business models.  Who pays for the increased cost of production? What's fair?
Technology requirements.  How does the book get printed?  How is it delivered?  How secure is the content?
Transition:  Let’s start with technology by first examining the economics of traditional printing.
I'm showing here how length of a print run affects cost per unit.  As the length goes up, cost per unit comes down.
Traditional offset equipment is very expensive, difficult to setup and hard to operate.  This leads to high cost per unit when you are doing short print runs – and requires a large team of individuals with very specialized skills – found, naturally, at printers.  This high cost of short runs is driven primarily by the cost of setting up the press and bindery.  This is driven by things like changing massive rolls of paper, changing the printing plates on the press, and tinkering with spine size adjustments on the bindery line.
These things all contribute to the cost of makereadies – getting the operation ready to print, not printing itself.
•
Transition:  With POD a new cost curve develops…
With appropriate management many of these factors that drive the steep curve can be minimized in digital printing. The new cost curve is flat – higher in the long run than traditional printer because of toner and paper waste, but much cheaper in the short run even down to runs of one.
•Digital printing eliminates the need for plate changes entirely
•Through appropriate scheduling and standardization, paper changes can be minimized, as can changes required by different trim sizes.
•Self adjusting and software-driven trimmers and binders can largely eliminate any tinkering on these items.  These binders and trimmers exist already and more are under development given the size of the market.
•Workflow integration can eliminate much of the effort of inputting orders, billing and internal to the manufacturing operation keeping the pieces together.
•
Transition:  Volume is also important – this is where the distinction of big iron POD and small iron POD comes in.
I’m referring to big iron POD as the world of central POD in which machines such as IBM InfoPrint 4000s and Xerox Docutechs live and the “small iron” world of cluster printing and, in the future, book vending machines exist.
Small iron allows in-store printing, either in front or back office
There are significant differences in the fully-allocated cost per unit in these different worlds depending on the total amount of units that are getting pushed through yielding a much higher ROIC for shorter runs.
I’m showing now how cost per unit varies with total  volume.  A big-iron system goes for $3mm fully-installed where a small-iron system can go for $40-60,000.  There are clearly capacity constraints on both, which are typically addressed by adding more equipment.
BTW, the main difference in VMC is the cost of paper waste.  The breakpoint happens at around 10,000 units per month.
Transition:  When you put the print run chart together with the volume chart, you get an overall map of where each type of printing is applicable.
There are two axes here:  print run length and volume, measured in something like total units per month across all titles.
This is a conceptual view – the lines are a lot more blurry and the regions aren’t these perfect rectangles
But the concept is valid
The breakpoint in print run is about 800 units. 
The breakpoint between a cluster approach and a big iron approach is in the neighborhood of  10,000 units per month.
Below 150 books per month, you can’t really justify any investment in printing equipment – you  just don’t have enough revenue to pay for the equipment.
Transition:  Clearly, volume is very important.  So how do we get this volume? We move into the world of content.
Volume is a function of number of titles and units per title printed at the same time at that location.  The key here is the number of tiles and the units per title per location driven by title attractiveness and traffic at the location.  I’m going to focus on number of titles for the moment and assume that there is some sales-based rationale for the selection of exactly which titles are made available.
Transition:  Different levels of the supply chain have very different volume profiles.
Publishers tend to fall into the traditional offset for most of their work – all except for low volume titles (galleys, ARCs, end-of-lifecycle or just plain slow-moving titles like academic monographs).  Because they’re seldom shipping directly to customers, this is more often than not a “make-to-stock” operation.
Distributors typically aren’t dealing with large demand for any particular title.  If they can justify printing to stock, it’s only on a limited basis.  Most of the printing will be done on-demand in units of one. Here printing *should* be done in response to demand or once the number of POD titles gets large, the operation will pay a very high price in terms of inventory investment.
Retail locations have the lowest demand for titles of all – except for the very hottest bestsellers, demand at a retail location for any particular title is statistical – this, BTW, is the cause of the terrible inventory turns you see for booksellers.  Here printing *must* be done in response to demand or there will be a very large inventory price to pay. 
Transition:  So what’s the moral here?
The current title base isn’t terrible exciting – it’s providing marginal economics for LSI – but only because they’re affiliated with Ingram – and Ingram gets most of the benefit. 
Publishers are still in a high-volume world – although they don’t have to be.
Retailers can do POD to the extent that look like a distributor – but they must be able to get titles.  Retail locations still aren't quite there.
Transition – this is changing, however.
Ingram had a devil of a time getting titles for their print-on-demand operation to start with.  Apparently as the concept has proven itself, university presses, trade publishers and many others are beginning to be able to see the benefits and are placing titles with them. At last count LSI had 15,000 titles, and it’s probably higher than that by now.
Transition:  This has a very beneficial effect on the ability of the participants at all stages of the supply chain to utilize POD technology.
Publishers are realizing that they’re missing sales and having to revert right because books are going out of print – this needn’t be so
Distributors and wholesalers are realizing that they can be generating revenue streams for themselves on their publishers through increase assortment without paying the inventory price to do it
With increasing title base and focus on the *right* titles, in-store POD is growing ever more practical.
Transition:
This is the existing pBook *physical* supply chain – how hardcopies of books actually flow.  Note that you can draw this from a content, rights, or even cash perspective and it will look different. Books move from printers to publishers to wholesalers to retailers to consumers and then between consumer as pass-along copies.  Naturally they can also skip steps, go backward and do all kinds of strange things.
Transition:  Each arrow here actually represents multiple transactions…
In fact what you see is the normal credit sale cycle of:  PO to shipment, shipment to invoice, invoice to payment, payment to remittance advice with 3-way match on the receiving end and receivables reconciliation on the shipping end. This goes all the way down the line to the cash sale made by the retailer to the consumer.
And this doesn’t even take into account the whole picture.  Between the publisher and wholesaler and between the wholesaler and retailer there is also a returns transaction loop that encompasses a variety of transactions.  In a civilized publishing world there are return authorization requests, return authorizations, return shipments and credit memos.  In the rough and tumble between the chains in the US and the publishers, the retailer tends to dispense with the returns authorization process and substitutes a deduction from the payment as the first shot in the returns loop.
Transition:  All of this leads to considerable cost…
Most of us are already aware that positioning inventory in advance of a s sale to a consumer, although required in order to hit reasonable service levels, leads to both inventory and returns in the channel.
Transportation costs are incurred with every hop – either forward or backward.
And naturally there are huge costs involved in processing all of these transactions.
Transition:  Central POD done at a distributor – that is using POD as an inventory management tool rather than a production tool – can alter this picture, mostly at the upper end of the supply chain.
In this illustration I’m showing the transaction flow for central POD. Note the simplification both up- and downstream for the publisher – at minimum you can just count the number of arrows going in and out of the publisher.  The printer is removed from the picture and there are now many fewer transactions between publisher and wholesaler
There is one conditional exception:  The publisher and retailer can, of course, make a decision on whether to stock the title at retail and make the book returnable – if this decision is made, then you don’t have much down-channel inventory reduction at all – only that which would be stored in the publisher’s warehouse. If this happens, returns can be just as much of a problem as you’d see with any backlist title.  I’ve taken them out in this picture. Also to avoid quibbling, let me acknowledge that the printer transactions are still largely there – they’re now just handled by the wholesaler, not the publisher.
The essence of this is that from an accounting perspective we have a transaction that looks more like self-reported subrights income than a traditional pBook sale.  Essentially we’re turning a credit sale into a cash sale or think of it as a consignment sale with no inventory at risk.
Transition:  If central POD simplified life for the publisher, distributed POD simplifies the entire supply chain.
In the distributed POD supply chain the files can reside at the retailer, the publisher or at a trusted third party.  In this diagram I have them at a POD wholesaler.  The consumer will typically approach an in-store POD kiosk with an attached browser.  The consumer will decide on a book, place an order at the kiosk, and make a payment to the retailer.  The book will be printed on demand and given to the consumer.  Payments will work back up the chain to the publisher with notice that a copy of the book has been sold.
Let me flip back through these slides so that you can see the difference.
Transition:  So let’s take a look at the overall implications of POD and distributed POD.
The big advantage to publishers and retailers is increased sales.  Focus groups *and* general agreement among retailers indicate that 90% of people if they can’t find a book they’re looking for, would rather leave than talk with a bookseller. Make to order reduces inventory and returns.  This is generally true of both kinds of POD, although with central POD there can be a temptation on the part of the retailer to keep stock on particular items. Down chain manufacturing – that is doing the POD manufacturing closer to the point of sale has a number of beneficial implications.  Distributed POD can result in higher costs – although this can be entirely offset by the pricing practice of the wholesaler. I’ve listed here some of the benefits. Finally, lower fixed costs associated with POD in general has the benefits of increasing the viability of low demand titles – this can mean the rise of self-publishing, but it can also mean the rebirth of the academic monograph.  Also with lower cost and the increase in delivery speed versus the old methods there comes the ability to increasingly customize content, such as what Booktailor.com is doing – I should call it “customerizing”
